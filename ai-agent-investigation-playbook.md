# How Investigations Actually Start — and How to Redesign Them for an AI-Agent Army

A field manual for building agent-driven accountability journalism on Substack, YouTube, and TikTok during the Epstein-files cycle and the 2026 election cycle.

---

## PART 1 — HOW HUMANS ACTUALLY START INVESTIGATIONS

### 1. The starting-point typology, with cases

Investigations almost never start from a blank sheet. They start from an **anomaly, a tip, a document, or a pattern** that an investigator decides is worth pulling on. Mapped to real cases:

**(a) Tip-driven.** A source walks in. Wirecard began in **summer 2014** when Australian short-seller / hedge fund manager **John Hempton** asked FT reporter Dan McCrum: "Hey Dan, would you be interested in some German gangsters?" That single sentence, plus a follow-up tip about acquisitions of obscure Asian firms, eventually produced the largest financial-fraud scandal in German history ([GIJN](https://gijn.org/stories/how-the-financial-times-exposed-a-billion-dollar-fraud/), [The Finanser](https://thefinanser.com/2024/03/how-the-wirecard-story-unravelled-an-interview-with-dan-mccrum-who-broke-the-truth)). Theranos similarly began with a **tip from a Missouri pathologist, Dr. Adam Clapper**, who blogged skeptically about Elizabeth Holmes's claims after reading a New Yorker profile; John Carreyrou later said, "Without Adam Clapper, I am almost 100% sure that I wouldn't have done anything" ([Dark Daily](https://www.darkdaily.com/2019/07/15/skeptical-missouri-pathologist-played-a-key-role-in-wall-street-journal-reporter-john-carreyrous-expose-of-medical-lab-test-company-theranos/)). Watergate began the same way: a Saturday-morning police call about a burglary at the DNC, picked up by veteran cops reporter Alfred E. Lewis, with novices Woodward and Bernstein assigned to chase it ([Washington Post](https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-srv/politics/special/watergate/part1.html)).

**(b) Document-driven.** An anonymous "John Doe" sent Bastian Obermayer at Süddeutsche Zeitung an encrypted message — "Interested in data?" — that grew into 11.5 million Mossack Fonseca files, 2.6 TB, and the Panama Papers ([ICIJ](https://www.icij.org/investigations/panama-papers/the-story-that-rocked-the-world-ten-years-of-the-panama-papers-part-1/)). The **Pentagon Papers** began when Daniel Ellsberg, after failing to persuade anti-war senators to enter the 7,000-page study into the Congressional Record, leaked them to NYT's Neil Sheehan in March 1971 — Sheehan then secretly copied them in Boston-area copy shops against Ellsberg's wishes ([Wikipedia: Pentagon Papers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon_Papers), [The Intercept](https://theintercept.com/2023/10/07/pentagon-papers-daniel-ellsberg-neil-sheehan/)). **Snowden** anonymously approached Glenn Greenwald as "Cincinnatus," moved on to Laura Poitras when Greenwald wouldn't install encryption, then handed thousands of NSA documents to Greenwald, Poitras, and Barton Gellman in Hong Kong in May–June 2013 ([Wikipedia: Snowden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Snowden)).

**(c) Pattern-anomaly driven.** Bethany McLean's "Is Enron Overpriced?" (Fortune, **March 5, 2001**) began with a contact telling her, "We can't figure out how they make their money. Let us know if you can"; she had a Goldman Sachs analyst background, found the public filings impenetrable, and said so in print ([Fortune](https://fortune.com/article/is-enron-overpriced-fortune-2001/), [Chicago Magazine](https://www.chicagomag.com/city-life/june-2019/bethany-mclean-has-a-new-podcast-for-media-junkies/)). McCrum's Wirecard work was the same pattern — "There are some technical things about it, and there are aspects of company accounts that you look for that don't quite make sense" ([The Finanser](https://thefinanser.com/2024/03/how-the-wirecard-story-unravelled-an-interview-with-dan-mccrum-who-broke-the-truth)).

**(d) News-event-driven.** The **Miami Herald's Julie K. Brown** revived a closed Epstein case in 2017–2018 that had been settled with a 2008 non-prosecution agreement; she identified ~80 victims and got eight on the record in "Perversion of Justice," published November 2018, which directly led to Epstein's federal arrest and Alex Acosta's resignation ([Wikipedia: Julie K. Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julie_K._Brown)).

**(e) Beat-reporter driven.** **Dominic Gates** had been on the Boeing aerospace beat at the Seattle Times since 2003; when Lion Air Flight 610 crashed in October 2018, he and a small team had the sources and technical fluency to investigate MCAS within months — winning the **2020 Pulitzer for National Reporting** ([Seattle Times](https://www.seattletimes.com/seattle-news/from-the-editor-why-it-takes-local-journalism-to-uncover-national-scandals-like-the-boeing-737-max-disaster/), [Wikipedia: Dominic Gates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominic_Gates)). The **Boston Globe Spotlight** team's Catholic-Church investigation began when new editor Marty Baron arrived in 2001 and asked the long-running investigative unit whether there was a *pattern* beyond a single priest, John Geoghan ([Columbia Case Study](https://ccnmtl.columbia.edu/projects/caseconsortium/casestudies/14/casestudy/www/layout/case_id_14_id_142.html)).

**(f) Power-mapping driven.** Activist research starting from "who has power here, how do they use it" — **LittleSis** / Public Accountability Initiative built #MapThePower in 2017 around drawing on William Domhoff's power-structure research and following corporate-political networks ([LittleSis](https://littlesis.org/news/learning-to-map-the-power-a-guide-to-our-2017-research-webinars/)).

**(g) Subject-driven.** A name keeps surfacing. **George Santos** is the canonical recent case: Long Island's tiny **North Shore Leader** (circulation ~20,000) noticed Santos's claimed net worth jumped from near-zero to ~$11 million in 2020–22, called him a "fabulist" and refused to endorse him in October 2022 — months before the NYT's December 19, 2022 bombshell ([PBS NewsHour](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/small-local-paper-uncovered-and-reported-george-santos-scandal-before-november-election), [Wikipedia: North Shore Leader](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_North_Shore_Leader)).

**(h) Money-driven.** "Follow the money" — IRE's defining maxim ([IRE](https://www.amazon.com/Reporters-Handbook-Investigators-Documents-Techniques/dp/0312135963)). Examples: tracing federal contracts on USAspending.gov, FEC filings, lobbying disclosures (LD-1/LD-2/LD-203 on lda.senate.gov), state-level donations on OpenSecrets ([USAspending](https://www.usaspending.gov/)).

**(i) FOIA-fishing.** MuckRock's model — file dozens of patterned FOIAs to many agencies and watch what comes back. MuckRock provides FOIA filing for as little as $20 for four requests and hosts the resulting documents on DocumentCloud ([MuckRock](https://www.muckrock.com/about/muckrock-101/)).

**(j) Dataset-driven.** Public dataset drops trigger investigations: **FEC bulk daily files** (electronic filings update every 15 minutes; ProPublica's Campaign Finance API mirrors this), the IRS Form 990 data behind ProPublica's Nonprofit Explorer (now over 1.9M filings), **OCCRP Aleph** (400M+ entities from 200+ datasets), ICIJ's Offshore Leaks database ([FEC](https://www.fec.gov/data/browse-data/), [ProPublica](https://projects.propublica.org/api-docs/campaign-finance/), [GIJN on Aleph](https://gijn.org/resource/using-aleph/), [ICIJ](https://offshoreleaks.icij.org/pages/data)).

**(k) Court-filing driven.** PACER monitoring via **CourtListener / RECAP**, which now offers RECAP Search Alerts ("Google Alerts for federal courts") covering "nearly every federal case," with API and webhooks ([CourtListener](https://www.courtlistener.com/help/coverage/recap/), [LawSites](https://www.lawnext.com/2025/06/courtlistener-launches-recap-search-alerts-for-pacer-filings-google-alerts-for-federal-courts.html)).

**(l) IG-report driven.** Inspectors General publish audits at **oversight.gov** (CIGIE's centralized site, with 23,816+ shared reports as of FY2022); HHS-OIG, DOJ-OIG, GAO publish weekly. These are pre-vetted leads ([Oversight.gov](https://www.oversight.gov/)).

**(m) Comparative analysis.** Compare like-to-like and find outliers. Spotlight built a list of 100 priests with suspicious "on-the-shelf" designations in church directories, cross-referenced via lawyers' Board of Overseers numbers across more than 1,000 cases over 15 years — a textbook database/comparative method ([Columbia Case Study](https://ccnmtl.columbia.edu/projects/caseconsortium/casestudies/14/casestudy/www/layout/case_id_14_id_142.html)).

**(n) Constituent-grievance driven.** **Outlier Media** (Detroit) runs an SMS service — text DETROIT to 67485 — that lets residents pull property records and connect to a reporter; the same flow surfaces patterns the newsroom investigates ([Outlier Media](https://outliermedia.org/txt-outlier/), [Nieman Lab](https://www.niemanlab.org/2018/03/by-mass-texting-local-residents-outlier-media-connects-low-income-news-consumers-to-useful-personalized-data/)).

### 2. Methodology frameworks

- **The 5 Ws** (who/what/when/where/why) is the kindergarten template. Useful for early scoping; insufficient for verification.
- **SMELL test (John McManus, 2013).** **S**ource (who is providing this?), **M**otivation (why are they telling me?), **E**vidence (what proof?), **L**ogic (do facts compel conclusion?), **L**eft out (what's missing?). Originally developed in the GradeTheNews.org / Stanford project, popularized in McManus's *Detecting Bull* and *Don't Be Fooled* ([MediaShift](http://mediashift.org/2013/02/dont-be-fooled-use-the-smell-test-to-separate-fact-from-fiction-online038/), [Zebra Fact Check](https://www.zebrafactcheck.com/john-mcmanus-smell-test/)). The "Left out" prong is the one most journalists skip.
- **Hypothesis-driven (ICIJ/ProPublica).** Start with a falsifiable proposition ("I think X is hiding Y money in Z jurisdiction"), then build a documents-and-sources campaign to disprove it. ICIJ's Panama / Pandora / Paradise pipeline used Nuix for OCR, Neo4J + Linkurious for entity extraction and graph visualization across 400 reporters in 80+ countries ([Linkurious](https://linkurious.com/blog/panama-papers-how-linkurious-enables-icij-to-investigate-the-massive-mossack-fonseca-leaks/), [Süddeutsche Zeitung](https://translate.google.com/translate?u=https%3A%2F%2Fpanamapapers.sueddeutsche.de%2Farticles%2F56febff0a1bb8d3c3495adf4%2F)).
- **Power mapping (RootsAction / MapLight / LittleSis / ACRE / Public Accountability Initiative).** Diagrams of relationships between people, money, organizations and political bodies. LittleSis's *Oligrapher* tool plus its training program "Map the Power" gives activists explicit how-to playbooks drawing on William Domhoff ([LittleSis](https://littlesis.org/toolkit), [LittleSis news](https://news.littlesis.org/2017/06/28/research-for-the-resistance-map-the-power-takes-off/)).
- **Steve Weinberg / IRE Reporter's Handbook approach.** "Follow the money — build a paper trail — document, document, document." Now in its 6th edition (Houston/Horvit) with chapters on collaborative investigations and social-justice beats; tied to IRE's Resource Center of tipsheets ([IRE](https://www.ire.org/new-investigative-reporters-handbook-now-available/)).
- **Follow the money / follow the documents / follow the patterns** — three IRE schools that map to source-driven, document-driven, and data-driven investigations.
- **Network analysis (OCCRP Aleph).** OCCRP indexes 1B+ records and uses graph relationships to surface intermediaries; co-founder Drew Sullivan's insight is that "organized crime is networked," so journalism must be too ([Tech Matters](https://techmatters.org/exposing-global-corruption-drew-sullivan-occrp/)).
- **Triangle of Verification.** Three independent sources for any contested fact. Carreyrou required corroboration beyond his initial single source "Alan Beam" before WSJ would publish ([Hatch Institute](https://thehatchinstitute.org/2018/05/21/an-interview-with-john-carreyrou/)).

### 3. Named exemplar investigations — how each actually started

- **Watergate.** A Saturday burglary, June 17, 1972. DNC counsel Joseph Califano notified Post editor Barry Sussman; Alfred E. Lewis got the lead byline; novices Woodward and Bernstein were assigned to follow up. Woodward attended the burglars' preliminary hearing where McCord admitted being former CIA; Woodward and Bernstein matched a phone number in the burglars' address book to E. Howard Hunt at the White House. **Mark Felt** ("Deep Throat"), then deputy director of the FBI, fed Woodward leads through a 2 a.m. parking-garage signaling system ([Wikipedia: Watergate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watergate_scandal), [Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/event/Watergate-Scandal)).
- **Pentagon Papers.** Ellsberg → Sheehan → NYT, March–June 1971; Sheehan secretly copied the docs in Boston copy shops, brought them to a Jefferson Hotel room and then the NY Hilton, where editors Rosenthal, Greenfield, Frankel and a writers' team organized them around the clock; Bagdikian then carried duplicates to the Washington Post ([Wikipedia: Pentagon Papers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon_Papers)).
- **Panama Papers.** Anonymous "John Doe" → Obermayer at SZ, late 2014/early 2015 → ICIJ → 400 journalists, 100+ outlets, 80+ countries, OCR'd through Nuix, entity-extracted through Neo4J/Linkurious, published April 3, 2016 with the world's biggest-ever data leak at the time (2.6 TB, 11.5M files). Recovered $1.36B+ in unpaid taxes per ICIJ tally ([ICIJ](https://www.icij.org/investigations/panama-papers/faqs/)).
- **Pandora Papers.** 2.94 TB, 11.9M records from 14 offshore service providers in 38 jurisdictions; same SZ/ICIJ collaboration model, published October 2021 with subsequent additions to the Offshore Leaks database in December 2021 and 2022 ([ICIJ Data Sources](https://offshoreleaks.icij.org/pages/data)).
- **Theranos.** Carreyrou read a New Yorker profile of Holmes on the subway in 2014; Adam Clapper's blog post then sparked his interest. Clapper connected Carreyrou to Richard Fuisz (a former Holmes-family neighbor), who connected him to former Theranos lab director "Alan Beam" (pseudonym; real name Tyler Shultz figured prominently elsewhere). Carreyrou's WSJ exposé broke October 2015 ([Hatch Institute](https://thehatchinstitute.org/2018/05/21/an-interview-with-john-carreyrou/)).
- **Wirecard.** Hempton tip 2014 → years of "puzzle" framing in FT Alphaville (avoiding the word "fraud" to evade UK libel law) → 2018 70 GB lawyer leak → "House of Wirecard" January 2019 → June 2020 collapse. McCrum used air-gapped laptops, surveillance counter-tradecraft, and won mother-of-whistleblower trust to flip a key insider ([GIJN](https://gijn.org/stories/how-the-financial-times-exposed-a-billion-dollar-fraud/), [Press Gazette](https://pressgazette.co.uk/news/dan-mccrum-financial-times-wirecard/)).
- **Epstein revival (Julie K. Brown, 2017–18).** Brown spent two years on what was officially a closed case, found 80 potential victims (some 13–14 at time of abuse), and got eight on the record. Won the 2018 Polk Award for Justice Reporting; the series triggered SDNY's 2019 federal indictment ([Wikipedia: Julie K. Brown](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julie_K._Brown), [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2024/01/04/1222857174/jeffrey-epstein-julie-k-brown-documents-clinton-trump-prince-andrew)).
- **Boeing 737 MAX (Seattle Times).** Gates was on the beat 16 years; after Lion Air, he had MCAS sourcing within months. Team won 2020 Pulitzer for National Reporting, 2019 Polk in Business Reporting, 2020 Loeb for Beat Reporting ([Seattle Times Pulitzer](https://www.seattletimes.com/opinion/editorials/pulitzers-highlight-democracys-need-for-a-free-and-robust-press/)).
- **Operation Varsity Blues.** Began with **Morrie Tobin**, a Yale-grad financier under SEC investigation for a pump-and-dump scheme. To get cooperation credit, Tobin told prosecutors that Yale soccer coach Rudy Meredith had solicited a $450,000 bribe to admit Tobin's daughter; Tobin wore a wire in a Boston hotel room in April 2018, Meredith took $2,000 cash and rolled, leading prosecutors to William "Rick" Singer and ultimately 33 parents and 9 coaches ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varsity_Blues_scandal), [Rolling Stone](https://www.rollingstone.com/culture/culture-news/operation-varsity-blues-scam-tipster-college-admissions-808418/)). The methodological lesson: **major scandals frequently cascade out of unrelated criminal cooperators**.
- **Trump Foundation (David Fahrenthold, WaPo, 2016).** Fahrenthold took Trump's claim of having raised $6M for veterans, saw the on-stage check totals didn't match, started calling charities one at a time, and crowdsourced via Twitter using photographed pages of his handwritten notebook. Eventually called 400+ charities; found one personal donation between 2008 and May 2016. Won the 2017 Pulitzer for National Reporting ([Poynter](https://www.poynter.org/fact-checking/2017/the-fact-checking-tools-david-fahrenthold-used-on-trumps-charity-claims/), [CNN](https://money.cnn.com/2016/09/13/media/david-fahrenthold-washington-post-donald-trump/index.html), [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2016/09/28/495782978/journalist-says-trump-foundation-may-have-engaged-in-self-dealing)).
- **Catholic Church (Spotlight).** Marty Baron arrived July 2001 and asked: pattern beyond Geoghan? The team built a database from 18 years of church directories (~900 priests), looked for "on the shelf"/"unassigned" status as a smoke signal, identified ~100 suspect priests, then cross-referenced through Board of Overseers numbers in 1,000+ court cases. Court-ordered document release in early 2002 confirmed the cover-up ([PBS NewsHour](https://www.pbs.org/newshour/show/the-boston-globe-challenging-the-catholic-church), [EBSCO](https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/law/boston-globe-reports-child-sexual-abuse-roman-catholic-priests)).
- **Hunter Biden laptop.** A masterclass in **multi-source verification problems**: NY Post (Oct 14, 2020) → 51 ex-intelligence officials' "Russian disinfo" letter → newspapers initially declining to verify → Washington Post forensic analysis (March 2022) authenticating thousands of emails via cryptographic Google signatures → CBS News verification (2022). The honest takeaway is what Poynter laid out at the time: *evaluate sources' biases AND interests, then look hard at proof, and treat the inability of multiple outlets to independently verify as a meaningful signal* ([Poynter](https://www.poynter.org/fact-checking/media-literacy/2020/is-the-new-york-post-story-about-hunter-bidens-laptop-legit-heres-how-to-form-your-own-judgment/), [WaPo forensic story](https://www.washingtonpost.com/technology/2022/03/30/hunter-biden-laptop-data-examined/)).
- **SBF / FTX.** **Ian Allison at CoinDesk**, Nov 2, 2022, published an article based on a leaked **Alameda Research balance sheet** showing Alameda's assets were heavily concentrated in FTX's own FTT token. CZ at Binance announced he'd dump FTT, FTT collapsed, FTX halted withdrawals Nov 8, bankruptcy Nov 11 — 9 days from balance-sheet publication to bankruptcy ([CoinDesk](https://www.coindesk.com/markets/2022/11/12/the-epic-collapse-of-sam-bankman-frieds-ftx-exchange-a-crypto-markets-timeline), [Wikipedia: FTX bankruptcy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bankruptcy_of_FTX)).
- **Trump-Russia / Mueller-era.** Timelines built primarily by combining (a) court filings (PACER, RECAP), (b) FEC and FARA filings, (c) congressional document releases, (d) leaks. Lesson: **timeline reconstruction is the dominant grammar of long political investigations**.
- **Surfside collapse (Miami Herald).** Won the **2022 Pulitzer for Breaking News Reporting**. Methodology: rapid-cycle document mining (the building's 2018 inspection by Morabito Consultants showed pool-deck waterproofing failure), engineer interviews, leaked photos from a pool contractor 36 hours before collapse, and forced production of records by other condo associations leading to closure of Crestview Towers ([Wikipedia: Surfside collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surfside_condominium_collapse), [GAO](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-24-106558)).
- **California family-law corruption.** The **Center for Judicial Excellence (CJE)** in San Rafael has documented family-court abuses since 2006, organized the first White House Briefing on the Family Court Crisis (May 2010), and produced the *Child Safety First* report cataloguing child homicides during custody disputes; CJE's 2016 audit campaign with Court Reform LLC forced the California State Auditor's review of the **Commission on Judicial Performance (CJP)**, which is the state's only formal channel for judge discipline and which admitted that **fewer than 2% of complaints by litigants result in any discipline** ([Center for Judicial Excellence](https://centerforjudicialexcellence.org/cje-projects-initiatives/ca-commission-on-judicial-performance-audit-reform/), [CJP](https://cjp.ca.gov/)). Public discipline data is searchable on the CJP database; private discipline summaries are also published, but most complaints remain confidential.

### 4. Activist and citizen methodologies (parallel to journalism)

- **LittleSis / Public Accountability Initiative**'s #MapThePower. Open, crowd-edited Wikipedia-style relational database; the Oligrapher visualization tool; trainings on FOIA, FollowTheMoney.org, SEC EDGAR, federal lobbying disclosure ([LittleSis news](https://news.littlesis.org/2017/06/28/research-for-the-resistance-map-the-power-takes-off/)).
- **Documenters Network (City Bureau).** Trains and *pays* community members ($16–$20/hour, $54+ per meeting) to attend public meetings and produce live tweet threads + templated notes — over 4,500 Documenters across 24 cities, more than $363,000 paid out as of 2022, 50,000+ scraped meeting documents on Documenters.org ([City Bureau](https://www.citybureau.org/documenters), [Documenters](https://www.documenters.org/about/), [Nieman Lab](https://www.niemanlab.org/2022/07/city-bureau-gets-10-million-to-make-public-meetings-more-public/)).
- **MuckRock.** Hybrid newsroom + FOIA filing platform (founded 2010 by Michael Morisy); merged with DocumentCloud in 2018; Pro accounts get embargoes. Its "Sunlight Research Desk" partners with newsrooms for bespoke records work ([MuckRock](https://www.muckrock.com/)).
- **OpenSecrets.** Federal/state campaign finance, lobbying (LDA), and the Revolving Door database tracking those who move between agencies and lobbying. Methodology requires that anyone in the database have *both* government and private-sector roles where they could plausibly influence policy ([OpenSecrets methodology](https://www.opensecrets.org/revolving-door/methodology)).
- **Bellingcat.** Full open-source toolkit at toolkit.bellingcat.com; methodology guides published since 2014; geolocation-of-imagery, network analysis, OCR, Hunchly browser-capture as forensic-evidence chain. Their published Ukraine-investigations methodology document explicitly references the **Berkeley Protocol** for OSI evidentiary standards ([Bellingcat toolkit](https://bellingcat.gitbook.io/toolkit), [Bellingcat methodology PDF](https://www.bellingcat.com/app/uploads/2022/12/JA-Manual-for-PUBLICATION.pdf)).
- **Citizen tracking projects.** Trump Accountability Tracker, conflicts-of-interest dashboards, Popular Information's worker-protection beat — Judd Legum's Substack with **523,000 free subscribers and 20,000+ paying** ([Press Gazette](https://pressgazette.co.uk/newsletters/substack-judd-legum-popular-information-journalism/)).
- **Whistleblower aid.** **Government Accountability Project (GAP)** — founded 1977; **Whistleblower Aid** (Mark Zaid, repped Frances Haugen and the first-impeachment whistleblower); **National Whistleblower Center**; **POGO**; **Empower Oversight** (EMPOWR); **WHISPeR / ExposeFacts**; **Whistleblowers of America**; **The Signals Network** ([whistleblower.house.gov](https://whistleblower.house.gov/whistleblower-support-organizations), [Government Accountability Project](https://whistleblower.org/), [Whistleblower Aid](https://whistlebloweraid.org/)).
- **Local accountability journalism.** **Outlier Media** (Detroit) SMS service; **City Bureau** Documenters newswire; the Knight Foundation–funded ecosystem of LION local independent online newsrooms.

### 5. How journalists pick targets

The decision is a **cost-benefit calculus** that experienced editors apply repeatedly:

- **Newsworthiness** (does the public actually need this?), **impact potential** (will it change behavior?), **evidentiary feasibility** (can we prove it?), **accessibility of records** (are docs reachable?), **legal exposure** (defamation/SLAPP risk).
- **"Smoke" indicators** that justify deeper investment: anomalous wealth growth (Santos's $11M), unexplained donor patterns, repetitive contracting awarded to politically connected entities, complex corporate structures with no clear business purpose, multiple-source whisper that the FBI is interested, sudden departures of senior figures, Inspector General references in passing to "ongoing investigations."
- **When to abandon.** When (a) you cannot get a second independent source, (b) the documents you'd need are sealed and unlikely to be unsealed, (c) the target's reputation already reflects what you'd publish (no marginal value), (d) your source's motivation contaminates the credibility, or (e) the legal cost dwarfs the public-interest value.
- **One-off vs. pattern.** A single bad act is news; a pattern is an investigation. Spotlight chose pattern over John Geoghan-as-individual; Brown chose pattern over Epstein-as-pedophile; the difference is what unlocks the systemic story.

## PART 2 — REDESIGNING FOR AN AI-AGENT ARMY

The user has a Mac M5 Ultra 128 GB running Qwen 32B and Llama 70B locally, plus periodic Claude Opus 4.7 synthesis, plus Playwright for sites without APIs, plus an outbound capability for email/voice/FOIA with a "judge agent" validating. The point of this architecture is to convert the investigator-time bottleneck — the actual binding constraint in human investigations — into a parallelizable monitoring + hypothesis-generation pipeline.

### 6. Ambient monitoring design — what should the army watch 24/7

Build each monitor as an independent Qwen-32B-driven agent with a deduplicated state store and a daily Opus synthesis pass.

| Source | Endpoint / mechanism | Refresh | What to watch for |
|---|---|---|---|
| FEC bulk + electronic filings | bulk download S3 / OpenFEC API; ProPublica Campaign Finance API mirrors, updates every 15 min ([FEC](https://www.fec.gov/data/browse-data/), [ProPublica](https://projects.propublica.org/api-docs/campaign-finance/)) | every 15 min | new committees, donations from previously dormant LLCs, conduit indicators, anomalous large donations near vote dates |
| Senate ID `lda.senate.gov` and `lda.gov` (replacement after June 30, 2026) — LD-1 / LD-2 / LD-203 | quarterly bulk; daily filings; REST API ([lda.senate.gov](https://lda.senate.gov/), [Senate downloadable databases](https://www.senate.gov/legislative/Public_Disclosure/database_download.htm)) | daily | new lobbyist registrations, new bill mentions, contributions reports |
| PACER via CourtListener / RECAP | RSS feeds from district courts; RECAP Search Alerts API + webhooks ([CourtListener](https://www.courtlistener.com/help/coverage/recap/)) | continuous | search alerts on names of interest, target entities, key statutes (FCPA, RICO, mail fraud) |
| Federal Register | REST API at federalregister.gov ([API docs](https://www.federalregister.gov/developers/documentation/api/v1)); RSS for saved searches | daily | new rules, new Significant rules under EO 12866, agency-specific notifications |
| Regulations.gov | REST v4 API ([open.gsa.gov](https://open.gsa.gov/api/regulationsgov/)) | daily | dockets, comment periods, comments themselves |
| SEC EDGAR | Full-text search + RSS feeds + real-time submission stream ([SEC.gov RSS](https://www.sec.gov/about/rss-feeds), [Bellingcat EDGAR tool](https://github.com/bellingcat/EDGAR)) | every 10 min | 8-Ks (cybersecurity/material events), Form 4 insider trades, S-1 registrations |
| GAO / IG reports | oversight.gov scrape ([Oversight.gov](https://www.oversight.gov/)) | daily | flags on agencies you cover; "open recommendations" still un-acted |
| State auditors | per-state scrape (Playwright) | daily–weekly | reports flagging contractor abuses |
| Cal-Access (CA campaign finance + lobbying) | nightly raw data dumps in tab-delimited tables ([Cal-Access raw data](https://www.sos.ca.gov/campaign-lobbying/helpful-resources/raw-data-campaign-finance-and-lobbying-activity)); MapLight Power Search overlay | daily | state-level conduit donations, lobbying spikes |

### 9. Content pipeline integration

**Substack.** Long-form investigative is what works. Judd Legum's **Popular Information** (~523K free, 20K+ paid, ~$740K/yr revenue per Press Gazette estimate) succeeds because of "in-depth original reporting" you can't get elsewhere ([Press Gazette](https://pressgazette.co.uk/newsletters/substack-judd-legum-popular-information-journalism/), [Substack: Grow](https://on.substack.com/p/grow-series-2)). Use a recurring "watch list" newsletter format. **Substack Defender** offers free pre-publication legal review and cease-and-desist response for U.S. professional users — enable this immediately ([GIJN: Substack](https://gijn.org/stories/how-investigative-journalists-are-using-substack-newsletters/)).

**YouTube.** Documentary-style (20–40 minutes), "how I/we investigated X" methodology content travels well — Bellingcat's audience growth in geolocation tutorials is the model.

**TikTok.** 60-second findings, hook in the first 3 seconds. Reuters Institute data and creator practice converge on: question hook, pattern interrupt, contrarian opener, or step-by-step format ([ICFJ on journalism on TikTok](https://www.icfj.org/news/i-blew-tiktok-journalism-heres-how-you-can-too)). The "investigator's notebook" format — show the actual sources, the actual document, the actual moment of finding — is what differentiates legit reporting from rumor.

**Verification standards (mandatory before publication):**
1. **SMELL test pass** by judge agent: source identified, motivation assessed, evidence cited, logic checked, what's-left-out reviewed.
2. **Two independent sources** for any contested fact (Carreyrou's WSJ rule).
3. **Hyperlink every claim** to a primary-source URL — and, separately, to an archived copy (Wayback Machine + Hunchly capture).
4. **No LLM-generated citation goes out unverified.** AI legal-research evaluations show even retrieval-augmented generation tools produce hallucinated citations at significant rates ([Stanford/Wiley study](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/jels.12413)); courts are sanctioning lawyers for this. Build a citation-resolver that fetches each cited URL, confirms the cited string actually appears, and rejects the post if any single citation fails.
5. **Every named individual** gets a notice-and-comment opportunity before publication where feasible.
6. **Defamation review.** For public figures, you need the **Bose** / *NYT v. Sullivan* standard: actual malice — knowing falsity or reckless disregard ([RCFP](https://www.rcfp.org/actual-malice-explained/)). For private figures, negligence in most states. Your judge agent must specifically check: (a) is the claim a fact statement vs. opinion?; (b) is it of and concerning a real person?; (c) is it false?; (d) what's the supporting record?
7. **Cease-and-desist response.** Substack Defender, Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press hotline, and pre-arranged media-defense counsel.

### 10. Validation strategy

**Backtest protocol:**
1. Pick a resolved case (e.g., Santos).
2. Reset the agent's knowledge to a date *before* the case broke (e.g., October 1, 2022).
3. Run the entire monitoring stack from that base; record everything the system flags.
4. Score: did the system surface (a) the candidate, (b) the smoke signal, (c) the right next step (e.g., "FOIA Brazilian court records")?
5. Calibration metrics:
   - **Precision** (of flagged cases, what fraction actually became scandals)
   - **Recall** (of cases that became scandals, what fraction were flagged)
   - **Time to detection** (how many days before the first major news story did the agent flag?)
   - **FOIA effectiveness** (response rate × usefulness of returned docs)
   - **False-positive cost** (how many person-hours wasted on dead-ends per real lead)

**First three runs the user should execute, in order:**
1. **Santos backtest** — public-record–only; pure paper trail; tests financial-disclosure parsing + Brazilian-court records integration.
2. **Menendez backtest** — tests state-court (NJ robbery records) + federal records integration; mid-difficulty.
3. **SBF/FTX backtest** — tests handling of leaked-document signals (the Alameda balance sheet) and time-pressured analysis.

Avoid Theranos as first run: the WSJ-quality source-development isn't easily replicable by an agent.

### 12. Mistakes to avoid

**Common investigative pitfalls:**
- **Confirmation bias.** You believe a hypothesis and stop looking for disconfirming evidence. Spotlight, McLean, and Carreyrou all describe building structured *attempts to disprove* their hypotheses. Hardcode an "anti-hypothesis pass" into the agent stack.
- **Single-source reliance.** WSJ wouldn't publish Carreyrou on a single anonymous source; Beam had to give him names. Build the rule that any claim with one source gets flagged "needs second source."
- **Source motivation contamination.** Hempton was a short-seller (had financial interest in Wirecard's collapse). Tobin was buying leniency. The Trump campaign brought the Hunter laptop. **All sources have motives. Disclose them.**
- **Burning sources before the story is ready.** McCrum waited *years* before publishing definitive fraud claims to avoid alerting Wirecard's countermeasures.
- **Tipping off subjects too early.** Madigan-Solis investigation worked because Madigan didn't know Solis was wired. Your agent's outbound emails / FOIAs eventually telegraph what you're investigating; sequence them so the subject of investigation is contacted *last*.

**AI-specific pitfalls:**
- **Hallucinated citations.** Mark Walters v. OpenAI established this is a real defamation risk; multiple court cases now sanction lawyers for filing AI-fabricated cases ([Damien Charlotin DB](https://www.damiencharlotin.com/hallucinations/), [Reason / Volokh](https://reason.com/volokh/2025/11/25/journal-of-free-speech-law-inevitable-errors-defamation-by-hallucination-in-ai-reasoning-models-by-lyrissa-lidsky-andrew-daves/)). Every citation must be resolver-checked.
- **Plausible-sounding false connections.** LLMs are pattern-completion engines and will infer "Person X probably knew Person Y" without evidence. Hardcode in your judge agent: "appearance in a flight log / contact book / mention of name is *not* evidence of crime; do not generate text suggesting it is."
- **Probability-bias amplification.** The GSU socio-technical analysis of the Epstein files ([GSU](https://sites.gsu.edu/krothschild/2026/02/20/a-socio-technical-systems-analysis-of-the-epstein-files/)) showed a single contact-book entry has near-zero diagnostic Bayesian weight; only multi-channel corroboration (flight log + financial transfer + sworn deposition) materially shifts the posterior. Your agent's prior must be calibrated this way.
- **Fabricated quotes.** Local LLMs invent direct quotes. Ban the agent from generating direct quotes that aren't substring-matched to a source document.
- **Anchoring.** When Opus does the synthesis pass, it can over-weight whatever the local LLMs surfaced first. Force a counter-pass that asks "what would *disprove* this hypothesis?"

**Defamation traps:**
- **Implied innuendo.** Stating two true facts in a way that implies a false third fact is actionable.
- **Headline–body mismatch.** Headlines that overstate the body's claim are a common loss path.
- **Failing to seek comment.** Even one round of "we asked X for comment, X declined" is a meaningful defense in showing absence of actual malice.
- **Republishing others' defamation.** Citing a tabloid claim doesn't immunize you. Verify or don't repeat.

**Operational mistakes:**
- **Premature publication of leaked documents.** Verify provenance and redact identifying info on victims (the Spotlight team's process).
- **Not securing your own infrastructure.** McCrum used air-gapped laptops and counter-surveillance because Wirecard hired private detectives. If you publish actual investigative work, assume targets will respond. Use Hunchly + cold storage for evidence.
- **Letting source-protection slip.** Sidney Zion publicly named Ellsberg as the Pentagon Papers source within days of publication, against Sheehan's wishes — Ellsberg was furious. Compartmentalize who knows what.
